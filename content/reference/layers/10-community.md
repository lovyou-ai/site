# Layer 10: Community

## Derivation

### The Gap

Layer 9 models dyadic bonds with depth, repair, and intimacy. But belonging to a community is more than having relationships with individuals — it's an emergent sense of home, of shared identity, of being part of something larger. The whole is more than the sum of its relationships.

**Test:** Can you express "This community has traditions that define it, shared resources everyone stewards, and a sense of home that newcomers gradually develop — and it celebrated a milestone together" in Layer 9? You can have attachments between individuals and reciprocity in pairs. But "traditions that define the community" (collective practice), "shared resources" (commons), "sense of home" (belonging), and "celebrated together" (collective joy) have no Layer 9 representation. Relationship is between two. Community is among many.

### The Transition

**Relationship → Belonging**

Dyadic bonds become collective identity. The fundamental new capacity: belonging to a collective that is more than the sum of its relationships — with shared resources, traditions, generational continuity, and collective celebration.

### Base Operations

What can a community DO that a collection of relationships cannot?

1. **Belong** — develop a sense of home and place
2. **Steward** — care for shared resources across generations
3. **Celebrate** — mark collective achievements and transitions
4. **Give** — contribute without expectation of return

### Semantic Dimensions

| Dimension | Values | What it distinguishes |
|-----------|--------|-----------------------|
| **Orientation** | Individual-in-community (one member's experience) / Community-as-whole (collective state) | Personal or collective? |
| **Temporality** | Present (current state) / Generational (across lifetimes) | Now or across time? |
| **Flow** | Inward (receiving from community) / Outward (giving to community) | Taking or contributing? |
| **Mode** | Practical (material, functional) / Symbolic (meaningful, ceremonial) | Concrete or meaningful? |

### Decomposition

**Group 0 — Belonging** (being part of)

| Primitive | Orientation | Temporality | Flow | Mode | What it does |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|------|------|--------------|
| **Home** | Individual | Present | Inward | Symbolic | The sense of place in a community |
| **Contribution** | Individual | Present | Outward | Practical | What each member gives |
| **Inclusion** | Individual | Present | Inward | Practical | Actively ensuring everyone can participate |
| **Tradition** | Community | Generational | Inward | Symbolic | Practices that define a community |

Belonging lifecycle: actors develop a sense of place (Home) → contribute to the community (Contribution) → barriers to participation are removed (Inclusion) → and shared practices accumulate (Tradition). Home is the subjective experience of belonging; Tradition is its collective expression.

**Group 1 — Stewardship** (caring for the whole)

| Primitive | Orientation | Temporality | Flow | Mode | What it does |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|------|------|--------------|
| **Commons** | Community | Present | Inward | Practical | Shared resources that belong to the community |
| **Sustainability** | Community | Generational | Inward | Practical | Can this continue |
| **Succession** | Individual | Generational | Outward | Practical | Passing stewardship to the next generation |
| **Renewal** | Community | Generational | Outward | Practical | How communities regenerate |

Stewardship lifecycle: shared resources are identified (Commons) → their long-term viability is assessed (Sustainability) → care is transferred to the next generation (Succession) → and the community regenerates (Renewal). This is the generational dimension of community — thinking beyond the present.

**Group 2 — Celebration** (marking what matters)

| Primitive | Orientation | Temporality | Flow | Mode | What it does |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|------|------|--------------|
| **Milestone** | Community | Present | Inward | Symbolic | Recognising significant achievements |
| **Ceremony** | Community | Present | Outward | Symbolic | Formal recognition events |
| **Story** | Community | Generational | Outward | Symbolic | The community's shared narrative |
| **Gift** | Individual | Present | Outward | Both | Giving without expectation of return |

Celebration lifecycle: achievements are recognised (Milestone) → formally celebrated (Ceremony) → woven into the community's story (Story) → and generosity flows freely (Gift). Unlike Exchange (Layer 2), gifts have no obligation attached — they strengthen community bonds without creating debts.

### Completeness Argument

1. **Dimensional coverage:** The {orientation, temporality, flow, mode} space is covered. The individual/community distinction ensures both personal belonging and collective health are modelled. The present/generational distinction captures sustainability and cultural transmission.

2. **Community studies coverage:** Tonnies' Gemeinschaft (organic community) maps to Belonging. Ostrom's commons governance maps to Stewardship. Victor Turner's communitas (collective joy) maps to Celebration. The gift economy (Mauss) is represented by Gift.

3. **Layer boundary:** None of these require concepts from Layer 11 (Culture). Community lives within its practices — it has traditions but can't reflect on *why* those traditions exist. Self-awareness about one's own cultural assumptions and the ability to see from outside one's own perspective are Layer 11's gap.
